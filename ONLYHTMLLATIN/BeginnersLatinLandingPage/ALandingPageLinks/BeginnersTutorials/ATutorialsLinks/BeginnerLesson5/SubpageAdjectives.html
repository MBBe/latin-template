Intro to Latin Adjectives

What is an adjective?

An adjective is a word used to describe a noun. For example:

"The big bear sat on the blue chair."

Here are some examples of adjectives in Latin:

Latin English
novum testamentum the new will
bonus dominus a good lord
predicta regina Isabella the aforesaid Queen Isabella
sancta Maria Saint Mary

An adjective:

  • Is normally in front of the noun it describes; sometimes it is behind
  • Agrees with the noun in:
    • Gender (masculine, feminine or neuter)
    • Number (singular or plural)
    • Case (nominative, vocative, accusative, genitive, dative or ablative)
  • Belongs to one of two groups (depending on whether it declines like first and second declension nouns, or whether it declines like third declension nouns do)

A bit more of an in-depth look at adjectives in Latin

This lesson covers adjectives that decline like first and second declension nouns.

Take a look at our example of novus, -a, -um (new):

Most first and second declension adjectives take these above sorts of endings.

Example: novam ecclesiam video means 'I see the new church'.

The above noun and adjectives are feminine accusative singular.

Example: parsona pueros novos vocat means 'The parson calls the new boys' in English.

The above noun and adjective are masculine accusative plural.

Although make sure to not assume that the ending of the noun and the adjective are always the same. Sometimes they are different, for example, novus agricola est (meaning 'He is the new farmer' in English) has both the noun and adjective in masculine nominative singular.

In the word list, first and second declension adjectives are written ‘novus, -a, -um’ This shows the three nominative singular forms:

  • novus is masculine and declines like dominus from the second declension
  • nova is feminine and declines like carta from the first declension
  • novum is neuter and declines like testamentum from the second declension

When you see an adjective written like this, you will know that it is first or second declension. You will be able to decline it using the same endings as novus.

Latin English
antiquus , -a, -um old
bonus , -a, -um good
dimidius , -a, -um half
dominicus , -a, -um demesne
magnus , -a, -um great, big
predictus , -a, -um aforesaid
quietus , -a, -um free, quit
sanctus , -a, -um saint, holy

Examples:

Latin English
ecclesia sancte Marie church of Saint Mary

ecclesia sancti Edwardi

(Edwardus, -i (m.) Edward)

 church of Saint Edward
ego dimidium manerium filiabus predictis domini do I give a half manor to the aforesaid daughters of the lord.
predicta domina tenet dimidium mercatum nova carta The aforesaid lady holds half a market by a new charter.
dominice terre domini sunt They are the demesne lands of the lord.
dimidiam marcam predicto agricole damus We give half a mark to the aforesaid farmer.

Look out for adjectives that end in ‘er’ in the masculine nominative singular.

Most lose the ‘e’ when declined, for example pulcher, pulchra, pulchrum (beautiful)

A few keep the ‘e’, for example liber, -era, -erum (free)

These are fully declined in the table of grammar
